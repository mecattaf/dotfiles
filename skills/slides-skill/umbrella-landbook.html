Introducing the Umbrella Handbook

Q1 Internal Operations Proposal

Thomas — Jan 20

What Changed

  • We have a new company handbook at handbook.umbrellalive.com — it's live and you can access it now.
  • Notion is being sunset; stop creating new pages there. The handbook is the new home.
  • This isn't just documentation — it's how we'll operate going forward.

Why We Built This

  • Most company handbooks are ceremonial — written once, rarely read, quickly stale.
  • We wanted something functional: a living system that encodes how we actually operate.
  • The handbook is that system. It's versioned, reviewable, and built to evolve.

The Problem With Notion

  • Notion encourages sprawl: anyone can create a page, so everyone does, and nothing is canonical.
  • No version control: you can't see what changed, when, or why — or roll back mistakes.
  • Not built for AI: agents can't reliably parse unstructured Notion pages to help us work.

What This Is

  • We built a company handbook deployed at handbook.umbrellalive.com — live now, accessible to the team.
  • It replaces Notion as our operating system for procedures, policies, and institutional knowledge.
  • Google Drive remains for static files (PDFs, legal docs). The handbook is for how we work.

The Structure

  • The handbook is organized into seven "tomes" — each covering a domain of how we operate.
  • Tomes are interconnected: values in People echo through Fundraising narratives and Sales positioning.
  • Every tome has a primary author responsible for keeping it current; all changes flow through review.

Tome 0 — Meta & Deal Room

  • How to use the handbook itself: reading paths, contribution workflow, what goes where.
  • Deal room: links to legal and financial documents (articles of incorporation, SAFEs, cap table).
  • Tech stack registry: every tool we use, who owns it, current state.

Tome 1 — People

  • Company values, mission, and vision — the foundation everything else builds on.
  • Hiring and separation procedures: job descriptions, interview process, offer templates, offboarding.
  • Policies and tooling: how we pay (Gusto), vacation policy, contractor setup.

Tome 2 — Fundraising

  • Historical pitch decks and the narratives behind them — what we said and why.
  • Investor FAQ: questions we've been asked, answers that worked, objections and responses.
  • Stakeholder CRM and update cadence: who we've talked to, what we owe them, when.

Tome 3 — Sales

  • Sales deck and Loom templates — the materials we use to close deals.
  • Scripts and punchlines: what works, objection handling, the language that converts.
  • Contract templates and customization guidelines for last-mile deal closing.

Tome 4 — Operations

  • Financial visibility: burn rate, runway, projections — always current, no spreadsheet archaeology.
  • Standup and retro structure: inputs (what we did) vs. outputs (what happened), weekly → monthly rollups.
  • Accountability tracking: commitments made in conversation get captured and followed up.

Tome 5 — Design & Brand

  • Voice and tone guidelines: how we sound to B2B clients vs. investors vs. end consumers.
  • Brand assets: logo usage, colors, typography — the visual identity system.
  • Landing page copy and marketing language: the words we use to represent ourselves externally.

Tome 6 — Engineering

  • Git conventions: semantic versioning, conventional commits, release-please automation.
  • Claude Code usage: how we expect the team to interact with AI tooling for development.
  • Current technical stack and tooling decisions documented for onboarding and consistency.

What This Means For You

  • When you need to know how we do something, check the handbook first.
  • When you want to change how we do something, propose it through the contribution flow.
  • When you onboard someone new, hand them the handbook URL instead of a 45-minute walkthrough.

How You Contribute

  • You don't edit the handbook directly — that's intentional, not a limitation.
  • Flow: draft in Claude chat → use Claude Code to create a PR → Thomas reviews and merges.
  • This keeps quality high, changes traceable, and ensures nothing breaks without review.

What Lives Where

  • Handbook: procedures, policies, living knowledge that evolves.
  • Google Drive: static files, legal documents, things that don't change often.
  • Slack: ephemeral communication, quick decisions, daily coordination.

What Stays the Same

  • Slack for quick communication and daily coordination — that doesn't change.
  • Google Drive for PDFs and static files — the handbook links to them, doesn't replace them.
  • Meetings, standups, retros — same rhythm, now with better documentation.

The Insurance Policy

  • If any one of us is unavailable, the handbook contains what we know — nothing is lost.
  • New hires get oriented in 15 minutes with a URL, not a week of shadowing and asking around.
  • Investors and stakeholders see a company that has its act together — disproportionate to our size.

This Will Scale

  • We're four people now. This is how 40-person companies wish they'd started.
  • Every procedure we encode is a procedure we never explain again — to a human or an AI agent.
  • The investment now compounds. Build it once, benefit forever.

What's Next

  • Your input needed: what's in your head that shouldn't be? What would a new person need?
  • Feedback welcome via the contribution workflow — this is a living system.
